About

A brain is a network of clocks, learning to keep time together

I am a PhD candidate at the Donders Institute for Brain, Cognition and Behaviour, Radboud University, in the final stages of a doctoral thesis on the nonlinear dynamics of large-scale brain function. My work treats the brain as a network of coupled oscillators poised near a Hopf bifurcation — the mathematical boundary between quiescence and sustained rhythm — and asks how proximity to that boundary shapes cognition, ageing, and recovery from stroke.

Modelling and stimulation play different roles in this work. Whole-brain computational models built on Stuart–Landau oscillators let me characterise and understand the dynamics already present in a brain — its distance to criticality, its repertoire of synchronisation patterns. Neuromodulation, by contrast, is not observational: techniques such as transcranial alternating current stimulation and transcranial ultrasound causally perturb those dynamics, letting me test whether the mechanisms the models describe actually govern behaviour, rather than merely correlate with it.

Before Nijmegen, I worked on virtual-brain infrastructure at the Institute for Bioengineering of Catalonia in Barcelona and on EEG biomarkers of stroke recovery at the National Brain Research Centre in India. Both roles left me with a lasting interest in translating dynamical-systems theory into tools clinicians and patients can actually use.

Research

Three ways of asking the same question

Characterise

Whole-brain modelling

Networks of Stuart–Landau oscillators, tuned near a Hopf bifurcation, reproduce the synchronisation patterns seen in resting and task EEG/MEG. Fitting these models to individual data reveals each brain's distance to criticality — a single dynamical fingerprint that shifts with ageing and after stroke.

Observe

Electrophysiology

EEG and MEG recordings, analysed through spectral and connectivity methods, provide the empirical repertoire that the models must explain — and the biomarkers that make dynamical theory clinically useful, particularly for predicting motor recovery after stroke.

Perturb

Neuromodulation

Transcranial ultrasound and transcranial alternating current stimulation causally intervene on brain dynamics rather than merely describing them. Targeting structures such as the lateral geniculate nucleus lets me test whether shifting a network's position relative to bifurcation actually changes large-scale synchrony, not just whether the two correlate.
